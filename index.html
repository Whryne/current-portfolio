About

More About Me

I am Big Mama Ne. I relocated to Madison, WI in the fall of 2020 to transition into a career in the non-profit sector. While I continue to seek my place in that field I was given the opportunity to gain knowledge and skills through the YWeb Career Bootcamp.

What's good?

One thing to know about me is I am a person of my word. I strive to move with compassion and integrity. It is important to me to create a lasting legacy and pave the pathway for those that will follow.

I am a 50yr old, Black, lesbian, womanist, mother, abolitionist and organizer. I am commited to Black liberation, which encompasses the liberation of LGBTQ liberation. My people are migrants of the Great Migration, factory workers, church folks, Black women, hustlers and addicts, dykes, studs, femmes, queens and all people fighting for the liberation of oppressed people. I work through a feminist lens and center, uplift, and value individuals/communities that are most impacted. I am working to ensure cleaner, safer, and equitable living conditions through mobilization and the leveraging of collective power.

Yweb Career Academy

Cohort 17

YWeb is an employment service program that trains women and people of color to become web developers/designers. The goal of the YWeb Career Academy is to increase diversity in this area of employment. Upon completion of the course, students will have opportunities to pursue full-time permanent employment, a paid internship, or a freelance career.

We prepare, position, and support individuals into life-changing careers that would otherwise be inaccessible. Our free to enroll, in-person program provides the technical and soft skills required to gain and sustain real employment. Then, we partner with employers and educational institutions to help students find internships, continuing education, or full-time employment depending on what is the best next step.

Team Sankofa

Black Lives of Unitarian Universalism

Team Sankofa provides thought leadership for the organizing and membership development strategies for BLUU. Sankofa literally means “it is not taboo to go back and fetch what you forgot.” After the term made its way to the United States, African American scholars commonly applied the term to mean “remembering our past to protect our future.” Team Sankofa started as a cohort of Black UU and UU adjacent folks who were attending the Allied Media Conference in Detroit and shared a common interest in social justice work.
